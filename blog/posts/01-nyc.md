As my most recent experience, I think I'll begin here. How did I get here?

## Background

This is my first internship. In the current state of Computer Science, it is hard to land a role. I was fortunate enough to have had research opportunities with one of my professors. If it weren't for that, I wouldn't be here. New York Life was pretty quick in their interview process and decision to accept me, and for that, I am grateful. I am grateful for everything that has come upon me in the past two years that have been able to turn my educational career a full 180.

## Why Computer Science?

I grew up poor, so I went a decent amount of my life without any technology other than CRT TVs with free cable. Eventually, I was able to have video games like the Gamecube, Wii, and PS2 like any other child — except by that time, everyone had moved on to the next generation of consoles. The first time I was able to get something on time was when the 3DS came out. I begged my father for one, and he was able to make it work. I was never great at video games, but I was definitely attached to them, and it's what sparked my interest in technology.

In high school, I knew I liked computers. I took a graphic design class and thought it was cool. I figured I'd choose the program that involves computers — graphic design. And that was it. The decision that would affect my future, made.

Luckily for me, at the same time, my high school began offering AP Computer Science — taught by one of my favorite math teachers. One who was able to turn my math grades from C's to A's. I enjoyed her style of teaching, and I like technology, so it was a no-brainer that I'd enroll. From the beginning, I fell in love with the course. We were working on code.org assignments and almost everybody was not interested. I liked it so much that I did it at home. The website had a leaderboard, and I was at the top. Miles ahead of everyone. My teacher told the class this was an exciting field and we should consider it for college. I instantly knew I had to change my major, and that's where it began.

## Realization

During most of my time in college, I was a C student in every aspect. I only received As in Computer Science classes as I still enjoyed them, and most math classes. I never really thought about my future, and for that, I didn't try hard enough to better myself. I thought since I was getting As for the classes that matter, I'd be alright. I was wrong, and I didn't realize until the end of my third year.

Just when I thought it was that time again to relax during the summer, I started hearing all these words: internships, experience, resume. I frantically searched up these terms only to find out I had been wasting my time. I quickly created a resume that showcased my barebones course projects, 3.2 GPA, and Chipotle Grill Cook experience. I started mass applying to internships, realizing that people who had far better resumes than I were also having trouble. This is when I found out how serious it was.

My first bit of luck came when I received an interview for Amazon SDE. I passed the OA and personality assessment. After that came the live interview — my first exposure to Leetcode-style interviews. I was asked a BST question, and I failed miserably. Right after bombing the interview, I searched for that question online, only to find it labeled as "Easy." I was truly humbled. So I decided to change things.

I decided to retake classes and do my best going forward. My second bit of luck was finding the LAEP program — as an economically disadvantaged minority, it was made for people in situations like mine. I applied to one assigned to a professor I had that semester. She recognized me from class and interviewed me. I got As in her course, and she accepted me. She later also asked me to be her grader.

This was the hardest part of my journey. During my last semester, I was grading, researching, working as a server at a retirement home with 12-hour shifts on Sundays, and helping my father with construction on Saturdays. Despite juggling all of it, I managed to come out on top. With the help of funding opportunities, I traveled to Hawaii with my sisters where I presented and published my first paper.

My professor was impressed and encouraged me to apply for the master's program and the Summer Scholars program. I applied to both. I was accepted into both. That summer, we dived into smart contract optimization and analyzed Sui and Aptos objects — squeezing out three published papers. All of this put together made my resume half-decent. When the time came to return to school, I applied for everything.

One day, New York Life sent me an automated invitation to apply on Handshake, so I instantly did. A behavioral phone interview. Then a video interview with a software engineer where we talked about my blockchain research experience. He then asked me to write a function that tells you if a string is a palindrome. Once I heard this, I was relieved — a company that doesn't treat an "Easy" problem like a gatekeeping trick. I passed, received the offer, and accepted it on the spot.

## Moving is hard

Between the acceptance and a few days before my flight, I felt nothing. No worries, no anxiety. I thought I was tough — I'd just be in and out. But when the day came, I felt sick to my stomach. The realization had settled in. I'm leaving, and I'm going to be alone for two and a half months.
